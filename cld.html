Numerical Integrators for Confined Langevin Dynamics

Joint work with B. Leimkuhler and M. Tretyakov Read on arXiv

Many applied problems across sampling, molecular dynamics, and optimization are naturally posed on constrained domains where $q \in \overline{G} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$[cite: 1]. The overarching goal is to design numerical methods that natively respect these boundaries rather than relying on ad hoc corrections[cite: 1].

Historically, numerical analysis has heavily favored reflected overdamped Langevin dynamics[cite: 1]. In that setting, the process is constrained via a boundary local time $L(t)$, resulting in a "push" in the position space along the inward normal direction $\nu$[cite: 1]:

$$ dX(t) = -\nabla U(X(t))dt + \sqrt{\frac{2}{\beta}}dW(t) + \nu(X(t))I_{\partial G}(X(t))dL(t) $$

Because of its utility, a rich body of literature has been developed for simulating these overdamped reflected SDEs[cite: 1]:

Methodology Numerical Idea
Walk-on-sphere Monte Carlo method for Neumann problem.
Projection methods Euler methods where tentative points are projected back to $\overline{G}$.
Penalty / barrier methods Approximate reflection by adding a large inward penalization near $\partial G$.
Lépingle / Milstein / Gobet Exact half-space procedures, change-of-coordinates, and boundary approximations.

The Gap: Kinetic Dynamics and Specular Reflection

In contrast to the well-studied overdamped case, the numerical integration of confined kinetic (underdamped) Langevin dynamics remains much less developed[cite: 1]. When we introduce momentum into the system, the boundary mechanism changes entirely: it acts as an elastic impulse upon collision, causing a specular reflection where the normal component of momentum is reversed instantaneously[cite: 1].

Building the Integrator: Splitting Schemes

To tackle this, we build integrators using splitting schemes composed of three fundamental operations[cite: 1]:

  • $A_c$: The exact collisional drift step ($\dot{Q}=P$) modeling free transport with elastic reflections.
  • $B$: The deterministic impulse step ($\dot{P}=-\nabla U(Q)$).
  • $O$: The stochastic momentum evolution (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process).

During the $A_c$ step, the implementation handles multiple collisions recursively[cite: 1]. Analytically, bounding the number of collisions is notoriously difficult. Instead, we bypass this by using a Lyapunov-type approach to bound the sum of incidence angles, $\sum(n_G \cdot \tilde{P}_i)$, which rigorously controls the local error without needing a strict upper bound on the raw number of collisions[cite: 1].

Combining these pieces, we can construct symmetric splitting schemes such as $[OBA_cBO]$[cite: 1]:

$$ P_{1/2} = O(P_k; h/2) $$ $$ \widehat{P}_{1/2} = P_{1/2} - \frac{h}{2}\nabla U(Q_k) $$ $$ (Q_{k+1}, P^r_{1/2}) = A_c(Q_k, \widehat{P}_{1/2}; h) $$ $$ \widehat{P}_{k+1} = P^r_{1/2} - \frac{h}{2}\nabla U(Q_{k+1}) $$ $$ P_{k+1} = O(\widehat{P}_{k+1}; h/2) $$

A Counterintuitive Result: The Midpoint Collision Effect

If we remove stochasticity and damping, the $[OBA_cBO]$ scheme reduces to the deterministic collisional Störmer-Verlet method ($[BA_cB]$)[cite: 1]. It is a known theoretical limitation that deterministic collisional Verlet generally loses its order, dropping to $O(h)$ accuracy when collisions occur[cite: 1]. Naturally, standard intuition suggests our stochastic scheme should suffer the same fate[cite: 1].

Surprisingly, numerical experiments show that symmetric stochastic splittings like $[OBA_cBO]$ maintain second-order weak convergence ($O(h^2)$)[cite: 1].

Why does higher-order behavior reappear? In the deterministic expansion, the order-losing error terms are proportional to $(2\tau - h)$, where $\tau \in (0, h)$ is the exact collision time[cite: 1]. These terms only vanish if the collision happens precisely at the midpoint of the step ($\tau = h/2$)[cite: 1].

By directly examining the statistics of the collision time in the stochastic setting, we find a powerful stochastic averaging mechanism at play. In expectation, the collision time naturally centers itself[cite: 1]:

$$ \mathbb{E}\tau = \frac{h}{2} + O(h^2) $$

Because the expected collision time aligns with the midpoint, the first-order error terms average out entirely[cite: 1].

Randomization in Deterministic Systems

This insight leads to a fascinating corollary[cite: 1]. We know the deterministic $[BA_cB]$ method degrades to first order because $\tau$ is fixed[cite: 1]. However, if we take deterministic Hamiltonian dynamics with elastic collisions and simply sample the initial conditions $(q_0, p_0)$ from a distribution, the collision time is no longer rigid from step to step[cite: 1]. The same averaging mechanism takes over, collisions occur near the midpoint in expectation, and second-order behavior is recovered[cite: 1].

Ultimately, while specular reflection creates complex collision dynamics, stochastic averaging ensures that suitable splitting schemes achieve second-order weak accuracy for confined kinetic Langevin SDEs[cite: 1].
