Numerical Integrators for Confined Langevin Dynamics

Based on joint work with B. Leimkuhler and M. Tretyakov
View Paper on arXiv (2404.16584)

Motivation and Background

Many applied problems are naturally posed on a constrained domain q ∈ G ⊂ ℝd[cite: 1]. Applications include sampling with truncated distributions or compactly supported priors, molecular dynamics where particles move in a container and collide with walls, optimization involving compact feasible sets, and solving PDEs through Feynman-Kac representations[cite: 1]. The overarching aim is to design numerical methods that respect these boundary mechanisms natively, rather than imposing ad hoc corrections[cite: 1].

Historically, a substantial numerical literature has been developed for reflected overdamped SDEs[cite: 1]. In these systems, the dynamics are constrained through a boundary local time, resulting in a "push" in position at the boundary[cite: 1]. Significant work by Lépingle, Milstein, Gobet, and others has explored walk-on-sphere methods, projection techniques, and penalty/barrier approximations[cite: 1].

In stark contrast, much less attention has been paid to the numerical integration of confined kinetic (underdamped) Langevin dynamics[cite: 1]. Kinetic Langevin dynamics incorporate both position and momentum[cite: 1]. When confined to a domain, the boundary mechanism acts as an elastic impulse at collision, causing a specular reflection where the normal component of momentum is reversed[cite: 1]. In Euclidean spaces, underdamped dynamics often improve sampling efficiency, and we expect related advantages in confined domains[cite: 1].

The Numerical Challenge: The Ac Step and Multi-Collisions

Integrating confined Langevin dynamics is fundamentally different from integrating overdamped SDEs[cite: 1]:

  • The boundary mechanism acts on momentum, not position[cite: 1].
  • Collision times must be explicitly detected inside a numerical time step[cite: 1].
  • Multiple collisions can occur during a single time step[cite: 1].

To address this, we construct numerical integrators using splitting schemes composed of three elementary pieces[cite: 1]:

  • Ac: The collisional drift step, which solves exact free transport with elastic reflections[cite: 1].
  • B: The deterministic impulse step[cite: 1].
  • O: The stochastic momentum evolution (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck)[cite: 1].

The Ac step can be implemented recursively if a path hits the boundary multiple times within a step of length h[cite: 1]. A major theoretical hurdle is that obtaining a deterministic upper bound on the number of collisions within one step is highly complex[cite: 1]. We overcome this by utilizing a Lyapunov-type approach to bound the sum of incidence angles, bypassing the need to explicitly bound the number of collisions[cite: 1].

Splitting Schemes and One-Step Error

Using these elementary blocks, we define first-order Euler-type schemes such as [PAC] and [AcP][cite: 1]. Under suitable assumptions, these schemes achieve weak order one convergence[cite: 1]. By bounding the boundary part of the local error using our collision estimate, the multi-collision issue is rigorously handled[cite: 1].

A Counterintuitive Observation: Recovering Second-Order Convergence

The story takes a highly interesting turn when we consider symmetric splitting schemes, such as the [OBAcBO] method[cite: 1]. When stochasticity and damping are removed, this scheme reduces to the deterministic collisional Störmer-Verlet method[cite: 1].

It is a known issue that the deterministic collisional Verlet method loses its theoretical order and generally degrades to first-order (O(h)) when collisions occur[cite: 1]. Based on standard stochastic numerics intuition, one might expect the stochastic collisional method to suffer the same fate[cite: 1].

However, numerical experiments reveal a counterintuitive result: symmetric stochastic splitting schemes like [OBAcBO] actually maintain second-order weak convergence (O(h^2))[cite: 1]!

The Midpoint Collision Mechanism

Why does the stochastic scheme recover second-order accuracy? The answer lies in the statistics of the collision times within the time steps[cite: 1].

In the deterministic collisional Verlet scheme, the order-losing error terms are proportional to (2τ - h), where τ is the exact time of collision[cite: 1]. If a collision were to happen exactly at the midpoint of the step (τ = h/2), these error terms would perfectly vanish[cite: 1].

In our stochastic setting, we discovered a stochastic averaging mechanism. Analytically and numerically, we observe that the expected collision time naturally centers itself[cite: 1]:

E[τ] = h/2 + O(h2)

Because the collision time is centered in expectation, the first-order error terms average out, allowing the [OBAcBO] scheme to achieve true second-order weak accuracy[cite: 1]. Interestingly, this same averaging effect can also make collisions occur near the midpoint in expectation for deterministic Hamiltonian dynamics, provided the initial conditions are suitably randomized[cite: 1].

Conclusion

While specular reflection introduces nontrivial collision effects—especially regarding multi-collisions—stochastic averaging acts as a powerful corrective force[cite: 1]. It enables properly designed symmetric splitting schemes to bypass expected theoretical bottlenecks and deliver highly efficient, second-order weak accuracy for confined Langevin dynamics[cite: 1].
