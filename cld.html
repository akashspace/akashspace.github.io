Numerical Integrators for Confined Langevin Dynamics

Joint work with B. Leimkuhler (University of Edinburgh) and M. Tretyakov (University of Nottingham) arXiv link

Motivation

Many applied problems are naturally posed on a constrained domain $q\in\bar{G}\subset\mathbb{R}^d$[cite: 1]. The overarching aim is to design numerical methods that respect the boundary mechanism rather than imposing an ad hoc correction[cite: 1]. This framework is highly relevant across several domains[cite: 1]:

  • Sampling: Truncated distributions, positivity constraints, compactly supported priors[cite: 1].
  • Molecular dynamics: Particles moving in a container and colliding with the wall[cite: 1].
  • Optimization: Constrained parameters and compact feasible sets[cite: 1].
  • PDEs: Specular boundary value problems through Feynman-Kac representations[cite: 1].

Numerics for Reflected SDEs

A substantial numerical literature has been developed for reflected (overdamped) SDEs, where the dynamics are constrained through boundary local time[cite: 1]. However, the numerical analysis of confined kinetic Langevin dynamics is much less developed[cite: 1]. Below is a summary of the well-established numerical focus on reflected SDEs[cite: 1]:

Work Numerical Idea
Walk-on-sphere Monte Carlo method for Neumann problem[cite: 1].
Projection methods Euler methods where tentative points are projected back to $\bar{G}$[cite: 1].
Penalty / barrier methods Approximate reflection by adding a large inward penalization or barrier force near $\partial{G}$[cite: 1].
Lépingle (1993, 1995) Euler procedure for reflected SDEs; exact in the half-space setting[cite: 1].
Milstein (1996) Change-of-coordinates scheme, Robin boundary value problems[cite: 1].
Gobet (2001) Half-space Euler approximation for simulation of diffusions in a domain[cite: 1].
Bossy-Gobet-Talay (2004) Symmetrized Euler scheme for efficient weak approximation of reflected diffusions[cite: 1].
Leimkuhler-S-Tretyakov (2023) Random-walk and symmetrized procedures for reflected diffusions, Robin problems, and ergodic limits[cite: 1]. Extended to second order[cite: 1].

Confined Kinetic Dynamics & The Numerical Scheme

In contrast to the overdamped setting (which uses a boundary push in position), underdamped/kinetic Langevin dynamics involve both position and momentum, leading to an elastic impulse (boundary jump in momentum) at collision[cite: 1]. This specular reflection creates new numerical tasks, notably that the boundary mechanism acts on momentum and collision times must be detected inside a numerical step[cite: 1].

To solve this, we construct integrators from three elementary pieces[cite: 1]:

  • $A_c$: The collisional drift step ($\dot{Q}=P$) with elastic reflections[cite: 1].
  • $B$: The deterministic impulse step ($\dot{P}=-\nabla U(Q)$)[cite: 1].
  • $O$: The stochastic momentum evolution ($dP=-\gamma P dt+\sqrt{2\gamma/\beta}dW$)[cite: 1].

Combining these yields symmetric splitting schemes, such as the $[OBA_cBO]$ method[cite: 1]. One step of this scheme looks like this[cite: 1]:

$$P_{1/2} = O(P_k; h/2)$$ $$\widehat{P}_{1/2} = P_{1/2} - \frac{h}{2}\nabla U(Q_k)$$ $$(Q_{k+1}, P^r_{1/2}) = A_c(Q_k, \widehat{P}_{1/2}; h)$$ $$\widehat{P}_{k+1} = P^r_{1/2} - \frac{h}{2}\nabla U(Q_{k+1})$$ $$P_{k+1} = O(\widehat{P}_{k+1}; h/2)$$

A Counterintuitive Observation: The Midpoint Collision

When there is no stochasticity and no damping, the $[OBA_cBO]$ scheme reduces to the deterministic collisional Störmer-Verlet method ($[BA_cB]$)[cite: 1]. It is well known that the deterministic collisional Verlet method is generally only first order when collisions occur[cite: 1].

Standard stochastic numerics intuition would suggest that the stochastic method should also suffer this order reduction to weak order one[cite: 1]. However, numerical experiments show that symmetric stochastic splittings like $[OBA_cBO]$ exhibit second-order weak convergence ($O(h^2)$)[cite: 1].

This is driven by a stochastic averaging mechanism connected to the collision timing[cite: 1]. In deterministic models, error terms are proportional to $(2\tau - h)$, where $\tau$ is the collision time; these terms vanish only if $\tau = h/2$[cite: 1]. In the stochastic setting, the collision time is approximately centered in expectation[cite: 1]:

$$\mathbb{E}\tau = \frac{h}{2} + O(h^2)$$

Because of this centering effect, the first-order error terms average out, allowing second-order weak accuracy for suitable splitting schemes[cite: 1].
