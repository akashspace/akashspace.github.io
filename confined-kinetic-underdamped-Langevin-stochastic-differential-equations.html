Numerical Integrators for Confined Langevin Dynamics

Joint work with B. Leimkuhler (University of Edinburgh) and M. Tretyakov (University of Nottingham) arXiv link

Motivation and Background

Many applied problems are naturally posed on a constrained domain $q\in\overline{G}\subset\mathbb{R}^d$[cite: 1]. Applications include sampling with truncated distributions or compactly supported priors, molecular dynamics where particles move in a container and collide with walls, optimization involving compact feasible sets, and solving PDEs through Feynman-Kac representations[cite: 1]. The overarching aim is to design numerical methods that respect these boundary mechanisms natively, rather than imposing ad hoc corrections[cite: 1].

Historically, a substantial numerical literature has been developed for reflected overdamped Langevin dynamics[cite: 1]. In these systems, the dynamics are constrained through a boundary local time $L(t)$, resulting in a "push" in position at the boundary in the inward normal direction $\nu$[cite: 1]. The reflected gradient SDE takes the form[cite: 1]:

$$dX(t) = -\nabla U(X(t))dt + \sqrt{\frac{2}{\beta}}dW(t) + \nu(X(t))I_{\partial G}(X(t))dL(t)$$

Because of its utility, this overdamped reflected setting has a highly developed numerical literature[cite: 1]:

Work Numerical Idea
Walk-on-sphere Monte Carlo method for Neumann problem[cite: 1].
Projection methods Euler methods where tentative points are projected back to $\overline{G}$[cite: 1].
Penalty / barrier methods Approximate reflection by adding a large inward penalization or barrier force near $\partial G$[cite: 1].
Lépingle (1993, 1995) Euler procedure for reflected SDEs; exact in the half-space setting[cite: 1].
Milstein (1996) Change-of-coordinates scheme, Robin boundary value problems[cite: 1].
Gobet (2001) Half-space Euler approximation for simulation of diffusions in a domain[cite: 1].
Bossy-Gobet-Talay (2004) Symmetrized Euler scheme for efficient weak approximation of reflected diffusions[cite: 1].
Leimkuhler-S-Tretyakov (2023) Random-walk and symmetrized procedures for reflected diffusions, Robin problems, and ergodic limits. Extended to second order[cite: 1].

Confined Kinetic Langevin Dynamics

In stark contrast, much less attention has been paid to the numerical integration of confined kinetic (underdamped) Langevin dynamics[cite: 1]. Kinetic Langevin dynamics incorporate both position and momentum[cite: 1]. When confined to a domain, the boundary mechanism acts as an elastic impulse at collision, causing a specular reflection where the normal component of momentum is reversed[cite: 1]. The corresponding equations are[cite: 1]:

$$dQ_t = P_tdt$$ $$dP_t = -\nabla U(Q_t)dt - \gamma P_tdt + \sqrt{\frac{2\gamma}{\beta}}dW_t + d\mathcal{R}_t$$

Here, $d\mathcal{R}_t$ represents the boundary jump in momentum[cite: 1]. In Euclidean spaces, underdamped dynamics often improve sampling efficiency, and we expect related advantages in confined domains[cite: 1].

The Numerical Challenge: The $A_c$ Step and Multi-Collisions

Integrating confined Langevin dynamics is fundamentally different from integrating overdamped SDEs[cite: 1]:

  • The boundary mechanism acts on momentum, not position[cite: 1].
  • Collision times must be explicitly detected inside a numerical time step[cite: 1].
  • Multiple collisions can occur during a single time step[cite: 1].

To address this, we construct numerical integrators using splitting schemes composed of three elementary pieces[cite: 1]:

  • $A_c$: The collisional drift step ($\dot{Q}=P$) with elastic reflections[cite: 1].
  • $B$: The deterministic impulse step ($\dot{P}=-\nabla U(Q)$)[cite: 1].
  • $O$: The stochastic momentum evolution ($dP=-\gamma P dt+\sqrt{2\gamma/\beta}dW$)[cite: 1].

A major theoretical hurdle is handling multi-collisions during the $A_c$ step[cite: 1]. Obtaining a deterministic upper bound on the number of collisions within one step is highly complex[cite: 1]. We overcome this by utilizing a Lyapunov-type approach to estimate terms involving the "summation of incidence angles," which rigorously bypasses the need to explicitly bound the raw number of collisions[cite: 1].

Using these elementary blocks, an example of a symmetric splitting scheme is the $[OBA_cBO]$ method[cite: 1]. One step of this scheme operates as follows[cite: 1]:

$$P_{1/2} = O(P_k; h/2)$$ $$\widehat{P}_{1/2} = P_{1/2} - \frac{h}{2}\nabla U(Q_k)$$ $$(Q_{k+1}, P^r_{1/2}) = A_c(Q_k, \widehat{P}_{1/2}; h)$$ $$\widehat{P}_{k+1} = P^r_{1/2} - \frac{h}{2}\nabla U(Q_{k+1})$$ $$P_{k+1} = O(\widehat{P}_{k+1}; h/2)$$

A Counterintuitive Observation: The Midpoint Collision

When there is no stochasticity and no damping, the $[OBA_cBO]$ scheme reduces to the deterministic collisional Störmer-Verlet method ($[BA_cB]$)[cite: 1]. It is well known that this deterministic collisional Verlet method loses its theoretical order and generally degrades to first-order ($O(h)$) when collisions occur[cite: 1]. Standard stochastic numerics intuition would suggest that the stochastic method should similarly suffer an order reduction[cite: 1].

However, numerical experiments reveal a highly counterintuitive result: symmetric stochastic splitting schemes like $[OBA_cBO]$ actually maintain second-order weak convergence ($O(h^2)$)[cite: 1]!

Why does the stochastic scheme recover second-order accuracy? The answer lies in the statistics of the collision times within the time steps[cite: 1]. In the deterministic $[BA_cB]$ scheme, the order-losing error terms are proportional to $(2\tau - h)$, where $\tau$ is the exact time of collision[cite: 1]. These terms perfectly vanish only if a collision happens exactly at the midpoint of the step ($\tau = h/2$)[cite: 1].

In our stochastic setting, we observe a powerful stochastic averaging mechanism[cite: 1]. Analytically and numerically, the expected collision time naturally centers itself[cite: 1]:

$$\mathbb{E}\tau = \frac{h}{2} + O(h^2)$$

Because the collision time is centered in expectation, the first-order error terms average out, allowing the $[OBA_cBO]$ scheme to achieve true second-order weak accuracy[cite: 1].

Randomization in Deterministic Hamiltonian Dynamics

This insight leads to a fascinating corollary regarding deterministic Hamiltonian dynamics with elastic collisions ($\dot{q}=p$, $\dot{p}=-\nabla U(q)$)[cite: 1]. While the standard $[BA_cB]$ method is only first order when collisions occur, what if we randomize the initial condition $(q_0, p_0)$ from a distribution[cite: 1]?

By randomizing the initial condition, the collision time is no longer rigidly fixed from step to step[cite: 1]. The very same averaging mechanism takes effect, causing collisions to occur near the midpoint in expectation[cite: 1]. Consequently, specular reflection creates nontrivial collision effects, but stochastic averaging allows second-order weak accuracy to emerge even here[cite: 1].
